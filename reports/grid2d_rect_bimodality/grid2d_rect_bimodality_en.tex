\section*{Executive Summary}
This report asks: \textbf{when a 2D domain is not $N\times N$ but a horizontal rectangle, under what conditions does the first-passage time (FPT) distribution become visibly double-peaked?}

We study two reproducible settings:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Two targets (absorbing)} at the two ends of the rectangle. We place the start closer to one end, design a short vs. long biased path (two timescales), and scan \textbf{rectangle width $W_y$} and \textbf{start position $x_0$} to build a phase map (Fig.~\ref{fig:tt-phase}).
  \item \textbf{One target with a reflecting-wall corridor}. Start and target sit near the two ends; reflecting walls form a corridor with local bias inside. We compare \textbf{with/without global bias} and scan $W_y$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:ot-phase}).
\end{enumerate}

Mechanistically, both constructions share the same template: the total FPT is the sum of two channel components; when the channel modes are separated and the weights are not extreme, the total curve shows two local maxima.

\section{Model Definition}
\paragraph{Rectangular domain}
\[
\Omega=\{0,\dots,L_x-1\}\times\{0,\dots,W_y-1\}.
\]

\paragraph{Base lazy random walk}
For interior sites,
\begin{align}
P((x,y)\to(x\pm1,y))&=\frac{q}{4},\\
P((x,y)\to(x,y\pm1))&=\frac{q}{4},\\
P((x,y)\to(x,y))&=1-q.
\end{align}
Reflecting boundary means out-of-domain probability mass is added back to stay probability.

\paragraph{Local-bias rule}
At biased sites, shift a fraction $\delta$ of stay probability to one arrow direction:
\[
\Delta=\delta\,p_{\mathrm{stay}},\quad
p_{\mathrm{stay}}\leftarrow p_{\mathrm{stay}}-\Delta,\quad
p_{\mathrm{arrow}}\leftarrow p_{\mathrm{arrow}}+\Delta.
\]

\paragraph{Global bias (optional)}
We adjust directional move probabilities by $(b_x,b_y)$ and renormalize so that the total move mass stays equal to $q$.

\section{Study I: Two Targets at the Two Ends}
\subsection{Construction and scan variables}
Targets are placed near the left/right ends on the midline:
\[
m_1\approx(1,y_{\mathrm{mid}}),\quad m_2\approx(L_x-2,y_{\mathrm{mid}}),\quad n_0=(x_0,y_{\mathrm{mid}}).
\]
We design:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a short path (fast channel) from $n_0$ to $m_1$,
  \item a long straight path (slow channel) from $n_0$ to $m_2$ (longer in the $x$ direction).
\end{itemize}
By default, we apply local bias only on these two one-cell-thick straight streams, so the arrows do not turn and the number of biased sites is small.
We scan $(W_y,x_0)$ and classify outcomes into phase 0/1/2.

\subsection{Mathematical mechanism (splitting decomposition)}
Treat the two targets as absorbing states and the rest as transient. With the block form
\[
P=\begin{bmatrix}Q & R\\0&I_2\end{bmatrix},\quad R=[r_1\ r_2],
\]
the splitting first-hit pmfs are
\[
f_1(t)=\alpha Q^{t-1}r_1,\qquad
f_2(t)=\alpha Q^{t-1}r_2,
\]
and therefore the total first-hit pmf satisfies the exact identity
\[
F_{\mathrm{any}}(t)=f_1(t)+f_2(t).
\]
Double peaks appear when the conditional modes are separated and the splitting weights are not too extreme; we operationalize this via a separation score, valley depth, and a minimum weight threshold.

\section{Study II: One Target With a Reflecting-Wall Corridor}
\subsection{Construction and scan variables}
Start and target are placed near the two ends on the midline. Internal reflecting walls are added on a central $x$-span so that a corridor forms around the midline. We add an eastward local bias inside the corridor, and compare three regimes: $b_x=0$ (no global drift), $b_x>0$ (drift toward the target), and $b_x<0$ (drift against the target), while scanning $W_y$.
To avoid confounding width effects with arbitrary placement, we first run a small anchor pre-scan over candidate $(x_s,x_t)$ and representative $b_x$ values, then fix one anchor and scan widths.
The selected anchor and the resulting clear interval are reported in Table~\ref{tab:ot-anchor} (script-generated to avoid manual transcription drift).

\subsection{Mathematical mechanism (two-channel absorption flux)}
For one target, the first-hit pmf equals the total one-step flux into the absorbing state:
\[
f(t)=\sum_{u\in\mathcal T} p_{t-1}(u)\,P(u\to m).
\]
Partition $\mathcal T=\mathcal C\cup\mathcal O$ into corridor-band vs outer-band sources, yielding the exact identity
\[
f(t)=f_{\mathcal C}(t)+f_{\mathcal O}(t),
\qquad
f_{\mathcal C}(t)=\sum_{u\in\mathcal C} p_{t-1}(u)\,P(u\to m).
\]
When the two components have separated modes and comparable weights, their sum can become double-peaked (Fig.~\ref{fig:ot-rep-fpt}).
