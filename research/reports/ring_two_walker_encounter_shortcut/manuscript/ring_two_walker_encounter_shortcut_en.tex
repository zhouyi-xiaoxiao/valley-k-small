(1) an implementation-ready and numerically robust verification of Eq.~(A1)$\Leftrightarrow$Eq.~(A8) from the appendix ``Product of two generating functions'';
(2) an exact two-walker encounter solver on a 1D ring with a directed shortcut, including a shortcut-channel decomposition that explains the observed double-peak regime.
In the present report, the production solver for the main encounter curves is the exact time-domain pair recursion; the A1/A8 block is the transform-domain compatibility anchor.

\section{Problem Setup}
We study two independent lazy biased walkers on the same periodic 1D ring.
Encounter first-passage is defined as
\[
T_{\mathrm{enc}}=\min\{t\ge1:X_t=Y_t\},\qquad
f_{\mathrm{enc}}(t)=\mathbb P(T_{\mathrm{enc}}=t).
\]
Walker A has a directed shortcut $\mathrm{src}\to\mathrm{dst}$ with strength $\beta$; walker B has no shortcut.

Default representative parameters:
\[
N=101,\ q=0.70,\ g_1=0.70,\ g_2=-0.40,\ n_0=5,\ m_0=55,\ \mathrm{src}=5,\ \mathrm{dst}=70.
\]

\subsection*{Directed Shortcut Injection (Selfloop Mode)}
Walker A uses the same shortcut convention as the ring shortcut reports:
\[
p_{\mathrm{sc}}=\beta(1-q),\qquad 0\le p_{\mathrm{sc}}\le 1-q.
\]
At node $\mathrm{src}$ we transfer this mass from self-loop to the directed edge:
\[
P_1(\mathrm{src},\mathrm{src})=(1-q)-p_{\mathrm{sc}},
\]
\[
P_1(\mathrm{src},\mathrm{dst})=P_1^{(0)}(\mathrm{src},\mathrm{dst})+p_{\mathrm{sc}},
\]
while all other entries follow the baseline lazy-biased kernel $P_1^{(0)}$. This keeps each row stochastic and preserves the interpretation of $\beta$ as shortcut strength.

\subsection*{Unified Notation and Metrics}
To make this first dedicated encounter report unambiguous, we fix all quantities used below:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item One-step kernels: $P_1,P_2$; non-absorbed joint law
  $J_t(i,j)=\Pr(X_t=i,Y_t=j,T_{\mathrm{enc}}>t)$.
  \item Two encounter times:
  \[
  T_{\mathrm{any}}=\min\{t\ge1:X_t=Y_t\},\quad
  T_{\delta}=\min\{t\ge1:X_t=Y_t=\delta\}.
  \]
  \item First-passage pmf and survival:
  \[
  f(t)=\Pr(T=t),\quad S(t)=\Pr(T>t)=1-\sum_{\tau=1}^{t}f(\tau).
  \]
  \item Detector traces used for peak/valley metrics:
  \[
  \bar f(t)=\mathrm{MA}_{w}[f](t),
  \]
  with fixed odd window $w=11$ for anywhere-encounter scans;
  for fixed-site K=2 diagnostics, use
  \[
  \bar{\widetilde f}(m)=\mathrm{MA}_{9}[\widetilde f](m).
  \]
  \item Peak/valley detector times (under one fixed smoothing+threshold protocol):
  \[
  \text{anywhere: }t_1=\text{first prominent local peak in }[t_{\mathrm{ignore}},t_{\mathrm{end}}],\quad
  t_2=\text{selected comparison peak in }(t_1,t_{\mathrm{end}}],\quad
  t_v=\arg\min_{t_1<t<t_2} g_t.
  \]
  \[
  \text{fixed-site K=2: run the same selector on parity index }m\text{ of }g_m=\bar{\widetilde f}(m),
  \text{ with }\tau\ge t_{\mathrm{ignore,pair}},\ \tau-t_1\ge\mathrm{min\_sep\_pair},
  \text{ no extra }t_{\mathrm{end}}\text{ cutoff, and reported times use }t=2m.
  \]
  \item Peak/valley intensity ratios (written on the detector trace $g$: anywhere uses $g=\bar f$, fixed-site K=2 uses $g=\bar{\widetilde f}$ with the $t=2m$ map):
  \[
  R_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau)=\frac{\min\{g_{t_1},g_{\tau}\}}{\max\{g_{t_1},g_{\tau}\}},\quad
  R_{\mathrm{peak}}=R_{\mathrm{peak}}(t_2),\qquad
  R_{\mathrm{dir}}=\frac{g_{t_2}}{g_{t_1}},\qquad
  R_{\mathrm{valley}}=\frac{g_{t_v}}{\max\{g_{t_1},g_{t_2}\}}.
  \]
  Candidate scoring uses $R_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau),R_{\mathrm{valley}}(\tau)$ to choose the late partner peak, then commits to $R_{\mathrm{peak}},R_{\mathrm{valley}}$ at $\tau=t_2$.
  By definition $R_{\mathrm{dir}}=g_{t_2}/g_{t_1}$ may exceed 1; all three ratios are reported as diagnostics, but the phase/onset gate itself is now based on peak separation rather than ratio thresholds.
  \item Phase labels: single / weak / clear (encoded as 0/1/2).
  We report two onset notions in parallel:
  the first shortcut strength with at least two prominent peaks (`has$\geq$2 onset'),
  and the first shortcut strength entering clear phase (`clear onset').
  \item Shortcut-channel shares:
  \[
  h_{\mathrm{sc}}(t)=\frac{f_{\mathrm{sc}}(t)}{f_{\mathrm{sc}}(t)+f_{\mathrm{no}}(t)},\quad
  H_{\mathrm{sc}}(T)=\frac{\sum_{t\le T} f_{\mathrm{sc}}(t)}{\sum_{t\le T}(f_{\mathrm{sc}}(t)+f_{\mathrm{no}}(t))}.
  \]
  \item Mass-conservation audit:
  \[
  \varepsilon_{\mathrm{mass}}=\left|1-\sum_{t=1}^{t_{\max}} f(t)-S(t_{\max})\right|.
  \]
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Mathematical Phase Definition (0/1/2)}
Let $g$ denote the detector trace ($g=\bar f$ for anywhere; $g=\bar{\widetilde f}$ for fixed-site K=2 coarse diagnostics).
Define prominent local-peak candidates
\[
\mathcal P=\left\{\tau:\ g_{\tau-1}<g_{\tau},\ g_{\tau}\ge g_{\tau+1},\ g_{\tau}\ge 0.01\max g\right\},
\]
plus detector-window constraints:
\[
\tau\in[t_{\mathrm{ignore}},t_{\mathrm{end}}]\ \text{(anywhere)},\qquad
\tau\ge t_{\mathrm{ignore,pair}},\ \tau-t_1\ge\mathrm{min\_sep\_pair}\ \text{(fixed-site K=2 coarse)}.
\]
If $|\mathcal P|<2$, no valid peak pair is available and we set `has\_two=False'.
We keep only candidates with $\tau-t_1\ge2$ so
$t_v(\tau)=\arg\min_{t_1<t<\tau}g_t$ is defined on a strict interior interval $(t_1,\tau)$.
Otherwise, we choose $(t_1,t_2)$ by the configured selector:
\[
t_1=\min\mathcal P,\qquad
t_2=\arg\max_{\tau\in\mathcal P,\ \tau>t_1}
\frac{(\tau-t_1)\,R_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau)}{R_{\mathrm{valley}}(\tau)+10^{-12}},
\]
where the fixed-site K=2 branch applies the additional parity-index constraint $\tau-t_1\ge\mathrm{min\_sep\_pair}$ and reports times after the $t=2m$ back-map to the raw clock.
If scores tie within numerical tolerance ($|\Delta\mathrm{score}|\le10^{-12}$), we break ties by larger $\tau$, then smaller $R_{\mathrm{valley}}$, then larger $R_{\mathrm{peak}}$.
with
\[
t_v(\tau)=\arg\min_{t_1<t<\tau}g_t,\quad
R_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau)=\frac{\min\{g_{t_1},g_{\tau}\}}{\max\{g_{t_1},g_{\tau}\}},\quad
R_{\mathrm{valley}}(\tau)=\frac{g_{t_v(\tau)}}{\max\{g_{t_1},g_{\tau}\}}.
\]
After selecting $t_2$, define
\[
R_{\mathrm{peak}}=R_{\mathrm{peak}}(t_2),\qquad
R_{\mathrm{valley}}=R_{\mathrm{valley}}(t_2),\qquad
\mathrm{sep}_{\mathrm{peaks}}=\frac{|t_2-t_1|}{w_{1/2}^{(1)}+w_{1/2}^{(2)}}.
\]
Here $w_{1/2}^{(1)},w_{1/2}^{(2)}$ are the half-widths at half-maximum of the selected peaks, measured directly on the detector trace $g$.
The phase code is
\[
\mathrm{phase}=
\begin{cases}
0,& \neg\mathrm{has\_two},\\
2,& \mathrm{has\_two}\ \land\ \mathrm{sep}_{\mathrm{peaks}}\ge 1,\\
1,& \mathrm{has\_two}\ \land\ \mathrm{sep}_{\mathrm{peaks}}< 1.
\end{cases}
\]
Anywhere primary detector settings are $(w,t_{\mathrm{ignore}},t_{\mathrm{end}})=(11,80,400)$.
Fixed-site coarse settings are $(w,t_{\mathrm{ignore,pair}},\mathrm{min\_sep\_pair})=(9,18,8)$ in parity-coarsened index.
The separation threshold is uniformly $\mathrm{sep}_{\mathrm{peaks}}\ge 1$.
The ratio quantities $R_{\mathrm{peak}},R_{\mathrm{dir}},R_{\mathrm{valley}}$ remain in the tables and figures as secondary diagnostics, and $R_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau),R_{\mathrm{valley}}(\tau)$ still appear in the candidate-selection score; they are simply no longer extra phase gates.

\section{Compact Derivation Roadmap: A1 to A8}
Define
\[
\alpha_k=1+s_k=1+q\left(\cos\frac{2\pi k}{N}-1\right),
\qquad
\widetilde P_{n_0}(n,u)=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\frac{h_k(n,n_0)}{1-u\alpha_k}.
\]
Insert into A1:
\[
I(z)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{du}{u}
\widetilde P_{n_0}(n,u)\widetilde P_{m_0}\!\left(n,\frac{z}{u}\right)
=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C\sum_{k_1,k_2}
\frac{h_{k_1}h_{k_2}}{(1-u\alpha_{k_1})(u-z\alpha_{k_2})}du.
\]
For each $(k_1,k_2)$, poles are
\[
u=\frac1{\alpha_{k_1}}\quad(\text{outer poles}),
\qquad
u=z\alpha_{k_2}\quad(\text{inner poles}).
\]
Choose $|u|=r$ such that
\[
\max_k|z\alpha_k|<r<\min_{k:\alpha_k\neq0}\left|\frac1{\alpha_k}\right|.
\]
Then only inner poles are enclosed, and residue summation gives
\[
I(z)=\sum_{k_1,k_2}
\frac{h_{k_1}(n,n_0)h_{k_2}(n,m_0)}{1-z\alpha_{k_1}\alpha_{k_2}},
\]
which is exactly Eq.~(A8).

\subsection*{Encounter Mapping (fixed-site and anywhere)}
The same A1$\to$A8 block is the core kernel for both encounter definitions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item fixed-site encounter at $\delta$:
  \[
  Q_{\delta}(t)=P_{n_0}(\delta,t)P_{m_0}(\delta,t),
  \]
  so A1/A8 applies directly at $n=\delta$.
  \item anywhere encounter:
  \[
  Q_{\mathrm{any}}(t)=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}P_{n_0}(n,t)P_{m_0}(n,t),
  \]
  hence
  \[
  \widetilde Q_{\mathrm{any}}(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C \frac{du}{u}\,
  \widetilde P_{n_0}(n,u)\widetilde P_{m_0}\!\left(n,\frac{z}{u}\right).
  \]
\end{itemize}
For the homogeneous defect-free ring, translation invariance lets the modal double sum collapse to a single sum:
\[
\widetilde Q_{\mathrm{any}}(z)=
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
\frac{\frac{1}{N}e^{i2\pi k(m_0-n_0)/N}}
{1-z\,\lambda_k^{(1)}\lambda_{-k}^{(2)}},
\]
where $\lambda_k^{(1)},\lambda_k^{(2)}$ are one-step mode eigenvalues of walkers A/B.
This collapse should therefore be read as a transform-domain validation block for the homogeneous benchmark.
In the main shortcut model studied later, walker A carries a local directed defect at $\mathrm{src}\to\mathrm{dst}$, so translation invariance is broken and we do not rely on the Fourier-collapse formula there.
All production anywhere-encounter figures with shortcut are generated instead by exact time-domain absorption recursion.

\section{Numerically Stable Verification Routine}
Use $u=re^{i\theta}$ and $du/u=i\,d\theta$:
\[
I(z)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}
\widetilde P_{n_0}(n,re^{i\theta})
\widetilde P_{m_0}\!\left(n,\frac{z}{re^{i\theta}}\right)d\theta
\approx \frac1M\sum_{j=0}^{M-1}F\!\left(re^{2\pi ij/M}\right).
\]
Implementation choices used in code:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item enforce contour feasibility with $r_{\mathrm{in}}<r<r_{\mathrm{out}}$;
  \item choose $r=\sqrt{r_{\mathrm{in}}r_{\mathrm{out}}}$ by default;
  \item adaptive doubling in $M$ from 16 upward until
  $|I_{2M}-I_M|<\varepsilon(1+|I_{2M}|)$;
  \item cross-check contour LHS against direct RHS (A8).
\end{enumerate}

Four sanity cases (real/complex $z$, edge $q=0$, small $|z|$) all pass with relative errors around $10^{-16}\sim10^{-18}$.

\section{1D Ring Encounter Algorithm}
Let
\[
J_t(i,j)=\mathbb P(X_t=i,Y_t=j, T_{\mathrm{enc}}>t).
\]
Exact update per time step:
\[
J_{t+1}=P_1^\top J_t P_2,
\qquad
f_{\mathrm{enc}}(t+1)=\mathrm{tr}(J_{t+1}).
\]
After extracting encounter flux, the diagonal of $J_{t+1}$ is set to zero (absorbing condition).
This gives deterministic, noise-free FPT curves.

A second coupled update tracks two channels:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item trajectories that have used the shortcut at least once;
  \item trajectories that have never used the shortcut.
\end{itemize}
Their sum equals the total encounter FPT and explains peak origins.

\subsection*{Mathematical Mechanism of Bimodality}
In this 1D ring encounter model, the double-peak regime is produced by superposition of two channel-specific time scales rather than numerical artifacts:
\[
f_{\mathrm{enc}}(t)=f_{\mathrm{sc}}(t)+f_{\mathrm{no}}(t).
\]
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{Early lobe:} shortcut-used trajectories dominate around the first peak (high $h_{\mathrm{sc}}(t)$), corresponding to shorter effective paths.
  \item \textbf{Late lobe:} no-shortcut trajectories take over in the mid/late window and generate the second peak scale.
  \item \textbf{Ring-topology amplification:} relative displacement and winding on a periodic ring increase delay/spread of the late channel, enlarging $t_2-t_1$.
\end{enumerate}
For the representative case (Table~\ref{tab:shortcut-rep}), we observe $t_1<t_v<t_2$ with threshold-qualified
$R_{\mathrm{peak}},R_{\mathrm{valley}}$; $R_{\mathrm{dir}}$ is reported only as a directional diagnostic (not used in phase thresholds).
Together with the channel handoff in the shortcut-share curve, this closes the mechanism chain for a clear-bimodal diagnosis.
The important nuance is that a weak double / multi-timescale structure is already visible at $\beta=0$; the shortcut does not create bimodality ex nihilo, but lifts an earlier fast channel above threshold and reorganizes the signal into a cleaner two/three-peak regime.

\section{Fixed-Site Encounter Extension (\texorpdfstring{$n=m=\delta$}{n=m=delta})}
We also complete the fixed-site encounter branch highlighted in your notes:
\[
T_\delta=\min\{t\ge1:X_t=Y_t=\delta\}.
\]
So we track two encounter definitions in one framework:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item anywhere encounter: $T_{\mathrm{any}}=\min\{t\ge1:X_t=Y_t\}$;
  \item fixed-site encounter: $T_\delta=\min\{t\ge1:X_t=Y_t=\delta\}$.
\end{itemize}

For fixed-site, define the free-process synchronous occupancy
\[
Q_{n_0,m_0}(t)=\Pr(X_t=\delta,Y_t=\delta\mid X_0=n_0,Y_0=m_0)
=P_{n_0}^{(1)}(\delta,t)\,P_{m_0}^{(2)}(\delta,t).
\]
Its generating function is exactly the appendix product-of-generating-functions object, evaluable by A1 contour integration or A8 modal double sum.
Then renewal gives
\[
\widetilde f_\delta(z)=\frac{\widetilde Q_{n_0,m_0}(z)}{\widetilde Q_{\delta,\delta}(z)}.
\]

Numerically, we implement exact time-domain absorption at a single point $(\delta,\delta)$ and apply K=2 parity coarse graining:
\[
\widetilde f(0)=f(0),\qquad
\widetilde f(m)=f(2m-1)+f(2m),\quad m\ge1.
\]
For first-passage traces, $f(0)=0$ so diagnostics effectively start from $m\ge1$; reported detector times use the right-endpoint map $t=2m$.

At baseline $N=30,q=0.7,n_0=5,m_0=12,\delta=0$, drift cases show:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item $(g_1,g_2)=(0,0)$ is unimodal;
  \item $(0.9,0.9)$, $(0.7,0.7)$, and $(0.9,-0.9)$ are clearly bimodal.

\subsection*{Multi-Peak Selection Rule (implementation-level)}
To avoid ambiguity when three or more prominent peaks coexist, we use the following deterministic rule:
Let $g$ denote the detector trace (anywhere: $g=\bar f$; fixed-site K=2: $g=\bar{\widetilde f}$ with $t=2m$ mapping).
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item Anywhere scan: smooth the raw first-passage sequence into $g$ with the fixed odd window, and keep candidate peaks in $[t_{\mathrm{ignore}},t_{\mathrm{end}}]$.
  \item Fixed-site K=2 coarse branch: run the same score-based selector on parity index $m$, with candidates constrained by $\tau\ge t_{\mathrm{ignore,pair}}$ and $\tau-t_1\ge\mathrm{min\_sep\_pair}$; this branch does not apply an extra $t_{\mathrm{end}}$ cutoff, and reported times are mapped back by $t=2m$.
  \item Set $t_1$ as the earliest prominent peak passing amplitude threshold.
  \item For each later candidate $\tau>t_1$, define
  (candidate pairs are restricted to $\tau-t_1\ge2$ to keep the valley definition interior)
  \[
  R_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau)=\frac{\min\{g_{t_1},g_{\tau}\}}{\max\{g_{t_1},g_{\tau}\}},\quad
  t_v(\tau)=\arg\min_{t_1<t<\tau}g_t,\quad
  R_{\mathrm{valley}}(\tau)=\frac{g_{t_v(\tau)}}{\max\{g_{t_1},g_{\tau}\}},
  \]
  \[
  \mathrm{score}(\tau)=
  \frac{(\tau-t_1)\cdot R_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau)}
  {R_{\mathrm{valley}}(\tau)+10^{-12}}.
  \]
  \item Choose $t_2=\arg\max_{\tau>t_1}\mathrm{score}(\tau)$; if scores tie up to numerical tolerance ($|\Delta\mathrm{score}|\le10^{-12}$), choose the larger $\tau$ (later timescale); if still tied, choose smaller valley ratio; if still tied, choose larger peak-balance ratio.
\end{enumerate}
This ``timescale detector'' keeps the late-timescale peak stable in multi-peak windows and prevents non-physical $t_2$ switching.

To localize which timescales and which sites carry the full encounter signal, we record the diagonal first-encounter flux
$\phi_t(n)=J_t(n,n)$ before absorption and define the site splitting probability
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:site-splitting}
\pi_{\mathrm{full}}(n)=\frac{\sum_t \phi_t(n)}{\sum_t f_{\mathrm{enc}}(t)}.
\end{equation}
This is the time-domain counterpart of Eq.~(10) in \cite{giuggioli2022transmission}: the marker variable $\rho_{\boldsymbol m_j}$ selects the target contribution in the generating function, while evaluating the corresponding first-encounter flux at $z=1$ yields the splitting probability over encounter sites.
For each peak basin $W_k$ selected by the timescale detector, we also define
\[
\pi_k(n)=\frac{\sum_{t\in W_k}\phi_t(n)}{\sum_t f_{\mathrm{enc}}(t)},
\]
and analogously $\pi_{\mathrm{yes}}(n),\pi_{\mathrm{no}}(n)$ for shortcut-used / no-shortcut channels.
The first three prominent basins are kept explicitly and the remaining mass is aggregated into `other'.

\section{Pitfalls and Debug Checklist}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{Contour crossing poles:} abort if $r_{\mathrm{in}}\ge r_{\mathrm{out}}$.
  \item \textbf{Near-singular amplification:} monitor $\min_{k_1,k_2}|1-z\alpha_{k_1}\alpha_{k_2}|$.
  \item \textbf{False early micro-peaks:} keep fixed smoothing and $t_{\mathrm{ignore}}$.
  \item \textbf{Truncation bias:} always report $\sum_t f(t)$ and survival tail.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Positioning Against Luca (Bristol) Literature}
This report places encounter dynamics into the same finite-domain random-walk first-passage framework used in Luca Giuggioli's line of work:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item The fixed-site renewal ratio
  \[
  \widetilde f_\delta(z)=\frac{\widetilde Q_{n_0,m_0}(z)}{\widetilde Q_{\delta,\delta}(z)}
  \]
  is consistent with multi-target/heterogeneous first-passage decompositions\cite{giuggioli2024multi}.
  \item The A1/A8 generating-function product route is compatible with defect/heterogeneity-oriented analytical strategies in related first-passage studies\cite{sarvaharman2020pre}.
\end{itemize}
So the encounter report is aligned with literature at the level of definitions, measurable diagnostics, and analytic-numeric closure, while adding a shortcut-channel evidence chain specific to this project.
